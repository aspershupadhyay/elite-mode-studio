## TITLE RULES — THE MOST CRITICAL ELEMENT

**WHAT A GREAT TITLE IS:**
- ALL CAPS. Every word.
- {TITLE_MIN_LEN}–{TITLE_MAX_LEN} characters. Hard limits. Count before submitting.
- One fact. One sentence. No double dashes. No second clause.
- MUST CONTAIN: one named actor + one specific action + one SPECIFIC NUMBER (dollar, %, count, date)
- The number is NOT optional. A title without a number is a failing title.
- Reads like a friend texting you the single most remarkable thing they just read

---

**RULE 1 — ACTOR RULE (most violated):**
The actor in the title MUST be the entity doing the thing — a country, company, person, or institution taking a real action.

NEVER let the outlet become the actor. "WSJ REPORTS X" means WSJ is acting. Wrong. The outlet never acts.
NEVER start with a source attribution: "WSJ REPORTS", "FT SAYS", "REUTERS:", "AP:", "BLOOMBERG:", "OFFICIALS SAY", "SOURCES REPORT", "ANALYSTS WARN".

These are wire-service filing leads. They belong in the caption with inline citation. They will never appear in a title.

CORRECT: "DJIA DROPS 800 POINTS AS FED SIGNALS JUNE PAUSE"
WRONG: "WSJ REPORTS DJIA FUTURES DOWN 0.23% TO 47241.00"

The difference: the correct title tells you what the market DID and WHY. The wrong title tells you which outlet repeated a tiny number.

---

**RULE 2 — IMPACT THRESHOLD:**
Every title fact must clear this bar: does it change something meaningful for money, safety, or the distribution of power?

FAILS THE BAR:
- A market index move of ≤1% (noise, happens every day, meaningless in isolation)
- A routine statement from a government spokesperson
- A trade talk opening, meeting starting, summit being announced
- A figure "reaching" or "approaching" some level without a structural cause

CLEARS THE BAR:
- A country doing something it has never done before (or for the first time in X years)
- A company or government committing a specific dollar amount above $1B
- A number that contradicts what the market, a government, or an institution claimed
- A shift in who holds leverage in a long-running situation

If the fact doesn't clear this bar, look deeper into the research context for the fact that does.

---

**RULE 3 — NUMBER QUALITY:**
A number in the title is required. But not every number earns its place.

MEANINGFUL NUMBERS: "$400B", "$12B", "18%", "3 MILLION JOBS", "147 NATIONS", "2025", "31-1", "FIRST TIME IN 40 YEARS"
MEANINGLESS NUMBERS: "0.23%", "47241.00", "1.2%", "0.8 PERCENTAGE POINTS" — these are precision without scale

Test: does this number mean something to someone who isn't already watching the ticker? If not, find a number that does — or reframe using a structural fact (a first, a reversal, a record, a threshold crossed).

---

**THE NUMBER REQUIREMENT — NON-NEGOTIABLE:**
Every single title MUST include at least one specific number. This is what separates a real fact from a vague claim.
- GOOD: "$400B", "18%", "3 MILLION", "2024", "31-1", "$200M", "147 NATIONS"
- BAD: "LAUNCHES", "STARTS", "OPENS", "BEGINS", "REACHES DEAL" — these have no number and are rejected

---

**THE ECONOMIST EDITOR TEST:**
Before submitting, ask: would the headline editor at The Economist approve this?
They would REJECT:
- Any title where the outlet (WSJ, FT, Reuters) is the grammatical subject
- Any title built on a sub-1% market move without structural cause
- Any title that starts with "OFFICIALS", "SOURCES", "ANALYSTS", "REPORTS"
- Any title where the "action" is a meeting, a talk, an announcement, a statement

They would APPROVE:
- A title where a named actor takes a specific, consequential, quantified action
- A title where the number represents magnitude, not just precision
- A title that reveals something most people covering this story buried or missed

**FAILING TITLE EXAMPLES WITH FIXES:**
